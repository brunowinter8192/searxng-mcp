A **metasearch engine** (or ) is an online [information retrieval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_retrieval "Information retrieval") tool that uses the data of a [web search engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_search_engine "Web search engine") to produce its own results. Metasearch engines take input from a user and immediately query search engines for results. Sufficient [data](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data "Data") is gathered, ranked, and presented to the users. 
Problems such as [spamming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spamming "Spamming") reduce the [accuracy and precision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accuracy_and_precision "Accuracy and precision") of results. The process of fusion aims to improve the engineering of a metasearch engine. 
Examples of metasearch engines include [Skyscanner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skyscanner "Skyscanner") and [Kayak.com](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kayak.com "Kayak.com"), which aggregate search results of online travel agencies and provider websites. [SearXNG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SearXNG "SearXNG") is a generic [free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_software "Free software") and [open-source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-source "Open-source") search software which aggregates results from internet search engines and other sources like Wikipedia and is offered for free by more than 70 SearXNG providers. 
## History
The first person to incorporate the idea of meta searching was [University of Washington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Washington "University of Washington") student Eric Selberg, who published a paper about his [MetaCrawler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MetaCrawler "MetaCrawler") experiment in 1995. The search engine is still usable as of 2024. 
On May 20, 1996, [HotBot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HotBot "HotBot"), then owned by [Wired](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wired_\(magazine\) "Wired \(magazine\)"), was a search engine with search results coming from the [Inktomi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inktomi "Inktomi") and Direct Hit databases. It was known for its fast results and as a search engine with the ability to search within search results. Upon being bought by [Lycos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lycos "Lycos") in 1998, development for the search engine staggered and its market share fell drastically. After going through a few alterations, HotBot was redesigned into a simplified search interface, with its features being incorporated into Lycos' website redesign. 
In 1997, Daniel Dreilinger published a paper on his experimental metasearch engine, SavvySearch, which was able to automatically select the correct search engine to prioritize based on prior experience.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasearch_engine#cite_note-Howe1997-10)
A metasearch engine called Anvish was developed by Bo Shu and [Subhash Kak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subhash_Kak "Subhash Kak") in 1999; the search results were sorted using [instantaneously trained neural networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instantaneously_trained_neural_networks "Instantaneously trained neural networks").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasearch_engine#cite_note-11) This was later incorporated into another metasearch engine called Solosearch.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasearch_engine#cite_note-12)
In August 2000, India got its first meta search engine when HumHaiIndia.com was launched.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasearch_engine#cite_note-13) It was developed by the then 16 year old Sumeet Lamba.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasearch_engine#cite_note-14) The website was later rebranded as Tazaa.com.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasearch_engine#cite_note-15)
